Four products. One label.

The Red Sky brief describes "an AI-driven private credit and underwriting platform designed specifically for experiential entertainment operators." The research across Layers 2–4 establishes that this single label fits at least four genuinely distinct products. Each has its own customer, revenue model, capital intensity, defensibility shape, and exit pathway. The differences span an order of magnitude on most dimensions. The decision is not whether to build Red Sky. The decision is which version, and on what sequencing.

Scope of this v2. Product, market, and financial architecture only. Operational sequencing, hiring critical paths, organizational structure, and political dependencies are deliberately stripped. Initiatives #2 (unified retail property data layer) and #3 (MarketScope, geo-based real estate analytics) are out of scope; acknowledged where the Red Sky data layer is a natural feedstock.

Finding 01 · Market
The gap is real but bounded.
Capital is institutionally available above $25M tickets (Cosm $250M / $1B post; Topgolf $1.1B to Leonard Green; AREA15 $161M; Great Wolf $1.55B; One Beverly Hills $4.3B+). Below that, in the $500K–$10M creative-led tier, capital is structurally absent in disciplined form. The underserved population is partly underserved because it is partly uncreditworthy.
Finding 02 · Defensibility
The data moat is the structural premium — and it takes years to build.
Vertical credit-data products trade at 8–15× revenue at category leadership (Trepp → Fitch ~$1B / ~11.8× est.; Placer.ai $1.5B at $100M+ ARR ≈ 15×). But Trepp = 47 years, Placer.ai = 12, CoStar = 25+, Cherre = 11. The path to defensible position is multi-year.
Finding 03 · Non-dominance
The four directions are non-dominated.
Each is the highest-scoring under at least one plausible weighting profile. D1 wins on speed-to-revenue and capital efficiency. D2 wins on direct alignment with the brief's structural framing. D3 wins on terminal exit multiple. D4 wins on DJM-asset leverage. The choice depends on what the team is optimizing for.

§2 · The structural problemCapital is shaped, not absent.

The problem Red Sky exists to solve is not that experiential operators can't access capital in absolute terms. It's shaped: capital is institutionally available at the top of the market, and structurally absent in disciplined form below it. The data needed to underwrite that lower tier is fragmented across ticketing platforms, payment processors, POS systems, attendance counters, and operator-disclosed financials. Aggregating it is hard. Without aggregation, no benchmark exists. Without a benchmark, every existing lender either declines, prices at merchant-cash-advance rates, or pushes risk onto the operator's personal guarantee.

Top of market · $25M+ tickets

Capital is fully available.

The institutional category has anchored capital partners across PE, REITs, hospitality bridge, and family offices. The deals are structured, the multiples are public, and the buyer universe is sophisticated.

The structural opening

The lender who first assembles the benchmark dataset can underwrite an entire tier of operators who today either cannot access capital or access it at MCA rates. That dataset is the moat. The four Red Sky directions are four different ways to capture it — selling the underwriting workflow to existing lenders (D1), running the credit book directly (D2), selling the data as a subscription (D3), or embedding credit on the ticketing/payments rail (D4).

§2 · The failure patternThe category produces winners and category-fatal losers in the same window.

Underwriting this tier without a category-specific framework is throwing darts. Operators that grew past their unit economics found insolvency, not just dilution. Borrower selection is the central operational risk for any direction that holds credit on balance sheet (D2, D4, D5a, D5b).

What the failure pattern means for underwriting

The Red Sky-target operators are not uniformly weak credits. But the format-failure pattern is real and not normally distributed — cost overruns and revenue misses in this category exhibit fat tails (per Flyvbjerg's 16,000+ project megaproject database: 92% over budget or schedule; 0.5% deliver on budget and schedule and benefits). v1 borrower selection determines whether Red Sky is a defensible specialty lender or another data-driven non-bank that gets bitten by adverse selection. The Bayesian + conformal methodology spine (§4) is built around making this fat-tail risk visible rather than averaging it away.

Three operator profiles concentrate the structural pain

Creative-led single-venue operators. Founder runs the concept; operations is lean; financials are a QuickBooks file. Needs growth capital, working capital, or refinancing out of MCA cycles. Ratio of potentially creditworthy to currently bankable is high.

Touring shows and pop-up experiences. Immersive Van Gogh sold 7M+ tickets across 21 cities and filed Chapter 15. Format is high-revenue, short-cycle, operator-execution-dependent. Underwriting requires category benchmarks against day-30 ticket-velocity, not annual P&L.

Multi-unit roll-ups in early-to-mid expansion. Five Iron Golf at 40 locations · Activate at ~55 · Immersive Gamebox post Harlan Capital recap (Feb 2025, 32 global locations) · Puttshack at ~20 with $329M raised. Capital exists at scale; disciplined credit fund at this tier does not.

§3.3 · The competitive landscapeNine adjacent categories. None fills the gap.

The Red Sky-target tier sits in a competitive blind spot — too small for generalist specialty PE, too large for community banks and SBA, mispriced by MCA, equity-only for family offices. Each adjacent category has structural reasons for not closing the gap. Those structural reasons are what makes Red Sky's opening durable.

1 · Generalist specialty private credit

Ares SME Opps · Apollo · Carlyle Global Credit · KKR · Sixth Street · Blue Owl
Why they don't fit. Minimum LP-driven deployment is $25M+ per facility. Below that is structurally not their problem. Ares SMEO Fund II $1B first close on $2B target 2024. Carlyle Global Credit $211.3B AUM YE 2025 — $4B+ deployed into sports/media/entertainment 2018–24.

2 · Generalist AI underwriting

Casca · Built AI · V7 Go · Numerated (→Moody's) · Ocrolus · Hyperscience · Zest AI
Why they don't fit today. All horizontal across SBA / CRE / commercial loans. None has experiential-vertical benchmarks. Casca $29M Series A Aug 2025 (Live Oak / Huntington / Bankwell customer-investors). Numerated → Moody's Nov 2024 (undisclosed, reportedly modest vs. $65B+ processed). The competitive risk: a generalist player could wrap the vertical in 24–36 months.

3 · Embedded transaction lenders

Stripe Capital · Toast Capital · Shopify Capital · Block / Square Loans
Why they don't fit. Wrong distribution rail — operators are not Shopify/Stripe/Toast merchants by default. Short-duration product shape only. But the architecture is the foundational reference for D4 (TicketSocket + MDM). Block / Square Loans >$200B cumulative through Jan 2026 at sub-3% loss rates across cycles.

4 · Community banks & SBA lenders

Live Oak · Huntington · Customers Bank · Pacific Premier · SBA 7(a) / 504 ecosystem
Why they don't fit. Underwrite to standardized templates without category benchmarks. SBA 7(a) volume $31.1B FY24; 504 capped at $5.5M/project. Decline what they can't pattern-match. The credit officer here is D1's natural customer.

5 · Ticketing platforms with embedded finance

Tixr · AXS · Vivenu · Eventbrite · TicketSocket · Spektrix · Patron Technology
None has built credit. Every ticketing platform sees real-time data on operator customers — exactly what Red Sky would want for underwriting. None has used it. The single largest dormant data asset in the landscape. Tixr ran ~$1B in sales 2025 (Dragoneer + Sony backed). Vivenu €32M Series C Aug 2025. Eventbrite → Bending Spoons Dec 2025 at ~1.7× revenue.

6 · Experiential REITs

EPR Properties · VICI Properties · Hines Mixed-Use 3.0
Why they're complementary, not competitive. EPR: $7.0B total investments, $6.6B (94%) experiential, 278 properties, 54 operators. VICI: 92+ experiential assets, $1.1B 2024 commitments at 8.1% weighted yield. Both originate sale-leasebacks at tens-of-millions tickets minimum. Natural partners for any Red Sky-tier platform.

7 · Merchant cash advance providers

The MCA market broadly
Why they're the problem, not the solution. Global MCA market projected ~$1.4T by 2028. Pricing destroys operator economics: 1.2–1.5× factor rates → 40–100%+ effective APRs. Many Red Sky-target operators are trapped in MCA cycles they cannot refinance out of.

8 · Family offices & equity crowdfunding

Mirasol · Avenue Sports · ROCK · Bolt · Eldridge · Access Industries · Kroenke / Wefunder / Kingscrowd
Why they don't fit the problem. Sports-owner FOs write equity, not debt — dilutive, not working capital. Equity crowdfunding small-ticket and retail. The instrument is wrong for the structural need.

9 · Institutional infrastructure

Trepp · Moody's Analytics · S&P Global · KBRA · Fitch · Verisk
No experiential dataset exists. Trepp covers CMBS; Moody's Analytics covers banking + CRE via Numerated; S&P MI covers broad market; KBRA rates structured credit. No published experiential methodology. The data-layer competitive risk for D3. Any of these could decide experiential is large enough to build for.
Why the gap persists despite being well-known

The data-aggregation problem is real and expensive. A defensible benchmark requires accumulating operator-level performance data across multiple ticketing platforms (no horizontal aggregator exists), multiple POS systems, multiple bank-statement formats, multiple operator-coined COAs, weather APIs, demographic services, and event-type taxonomies. Multi-year integration effort.

The operator population's data-sharing willingness is unknown. Operators historically protect this data. Ticketing platforms are viewed as utilities, not data partners. The "give to get" value proposition has not been tested at scale.

The tier is small enough that generalists pass and large enough that vertical specialists never get scale. $0.5–3B annual originations is a category but structurally below institutional ticket-size thresholds. Empirical evidence: no dedicated experiential-credit fund of meaningful size has closed in 2023–2026. CalPERS Q4 2024 ($20.5B private market commitments) and CalSTRS H1 2025 ($5.2B) disclosed no experiential line item. New Mexico SIC's commitment to Ares SMEO II + Meow Wolf is the closest LP signal, routed through generalist platforms.

§4 · The cross-cutting productSix capabilities. One spine.

Whatever direction Red Sky takes, six capabilities are load-bearing. They are the spine of the product. The depth at which each is built varies by direction (§4.7); the existence of each is universal. The choices below are not arbitrary — each capability is grounded in adjacent reference systems (FinRegLab's 2023 empirical study, Flyvbjerg's megaproject database, Stanford RegLab's 2024 legal-AI hallucination study, the Arctos / Ross-Michigan small-N franchise-valuation methodology).

Capability 01

Credit analytics methodology

A model that ingests operator data, produces a credit-risk assessment, and exposes the reasoning. Required: explainable, calibrated, produces uncertainty bands that widen at small N, supports cold-start strategies for sub-verticals where data is sparse.

The architectural choice with the right properties for Red Sky's small-N high-noise regime is Bayesian hierarchical modeling with conformal prediction on top. The Bayesian layer produces principled point estimates plus credible intervals; the conformal wrapper provides distribution-free coverage guarantees that don't depend on the prior being correctly specified. The failure mode at small N is widening credible intervals (informative) rather than overconfident wrong point estimates (misleading) — which is the worst possible property for adverse-action defense.

Capability 02

Projection validation filter

The feature Cooper named on May 6: a financial and statistical review layer that compares operator-submitted forecasts against market data, historical performance, comparable venues, and probability models. Required: explainable, auditable, surfaces specific overstated assumptions.

No single validation method is sufficient. A defensible filter is a stack: reverse stress testing ("what would have to be true for the projection to hold?" — the 65%-miss anecdote Cooper cited was this in plain language); reference-class forecasting (Flyvbjerg's iron law: 92% of megaprojects come in over budget or schedule); comparable cohort benchmarking against the Red Sky-built panel; and sensitivity / scenario modeling tied to the underlying Bayesian credible intervals.

Capability 03

ML-assisted synthesis with citation discipline

Credit memo generation, scenario modeling, portfolio narratives — turning structured + unstructured data into long-form prose a credit committee will sign. Modular / agentic RAG with hybrid retrieval, hierarchical+proposition chunking, multi-step planner→retriever→extractor→writer.

The hallucination problem is the structural risk. Stanford RegLab 2024 found leading legal-AI products hallucinate on 17–33% of queries; Harvey reports 0.2% on tasks specifically tuned for. Production targets: ≥99% pointer-integrity pass rate, ≥95% claim-evidence alignment, hard refusal on residual. Anything weaker is not a credit product. Buy the synthesis layer (Anthropic Citations API + Hebbia / Brightwave class), build the data ingestion.

Capability 04

Operator data integration architecture

Ingest across at least eight upstream types: bank, accounting (GL/AR/AP), ticketing, POS, payments, CRM, payroll, documents. Lakehouse-anchored (Snowflake / Databricks) with medallion bronze/silver/gold pattern; graph + vector are v2/v3.

Capability 05

Risk modeling & uncertainty quantification

Beyond the credit-analytics layer: stress-test outputs (CCAR-pattern scenario modeling for institutional-grade reporting), portfolio-level expected loss with credible intervals, and a refusal-aware decision layer that says "we don't know enough to score this" rather than producing an overconfident point estimate.

Refusal is a feature, not a failure. The Bayesian + conformal architecture supports this directly — when the credible interval is wider than the lender's risk-tolerance threshold, the product refuses rather than scores. This is the structural protection against adverse-selection failure modes the market has experienced (Pinstripes, Lighthouse, Sandbox).

Capability 06

Portfolio monitoring

Near-real-time (daily-to-hourly) ingestion, not true streaming. Streaming infrastructure is wrong for a $500K–$10M loan book. Covenant tracking patterned on CREFC IRP v8.4 (the CMBS standard). Rule-based MVP for v1; STL + Prophet anomaly detection v2; ML anomaly v3. Three-audience UX: operator-facing compliance · internal portfolio · capital-partner-facing pack.

The "deal flywheel" is real but bounded. Block's stable sub-3% loss rate across $200B is the strongest evidence that continuous-data underwriting improves through cycles. But the mechanism is data scale, not portfolio size. A realistic 18-month portfolio of 10–20 operators is too small to retrain a credit model usefully. The flywheel kicks in around Year 3, not Year 1. Buy the fund-admin shell (Allvue / Solovis tier ~$50–200K/yr); build the operator-data ingestion and experiential-specific covenant logic on top.

▌ Direction 2 · Red Sky Credit Platform

The credit infrastructure as a fund.

"A purpose-built underwriting + portfolio-monitoring system whose v1 customer is a credit committee running an experiential-credit vehicle."

Realistic ceiling
$90–230M
7–10 yr · franchise sale + fund econ
What it is

A full-stack credit infrastructure: underwriting + portfolio monitoring + ongoing operator visibility, deployed in service of a credit vehicle that holds experiential-operator credit risk on a managed-fund basis. The product is both the system that does the underwriting and the fund whose portfolio the system underwrites.

Who pays

v1 customer is the affiliated credit committee. Capital partners (institutional LPs, family offices, sponsor co-invest) review the committee's work during diligence and quarterly performance reviews. The full LP-facing reporting infrastructure is part of the product.

What's the structural opening

No dedicated experiential-credit fund of meaningful size has closed in 2023–2026. Despite the institutionalization of the category (Cosm, AREA15, Sphere, Topgolf, Great Wolf, One Beverly Hills), capital reaches the category through generalist sports/M&E platforms and real-estate vehicles. Never through a thesis-specific credit fund. CalPERS Q4 2024 and CalSTRS H1 2025 disclosed no experiential allocation. The infrastructure layer Cooper's brief explicitly names does not exist.

Why this matches the brief most directly

The brief says "a scalable infrastructure layer for structured credit in experiential entertainment" (foundation §01 line 12). The brief's long-term vision — institutional lending marketplaces, dynamic revenue-based financing, asset-level securitization, AI-assisted capital structuring — is the language of a fund / capital-markets platform, not a SaaS product. D2 is the brief's primary anchor.

What it is

A longitudinal, institutionally-credible benchmark dataset on experiential operator performance — attendance, ticket-yield, opex structure, ramp curves, failure modes, sub-vertical-level base rates. Sold as a subscription. The product is the dataset and the analytics around it, not the underwriting decision.

Who pays

Institutional buyers of vertical-credit data: ratings agencies (Moody's, S&P, Fitch post-Trepp, KBRA); CRE intelligence (CoStar, Cherre, MSCI RCA, Green Street); institutional lenders (Ares, Carlyle, KKR, Sixth Street, Apollo, Blue Owl); experiential REITs (EPR, VICI); family offices; operators themselves at v3+ (benchmark-your-venue product).

The dependency observation

D3 is downstream of D2 in the strategic dependency graph. D3 needs proprietary longitudinal operator-performance data — which is most naturally produced by running a credit book (D2) that accumulates the data through borrower-permissioned data-sharing. D3 as a primary direction without a D2-equivalent data-acquisition strategy means launching a data product with no proprietary data, which collapses D3 to a $5–15M industry-research-publication business, not a $50M+ Trepp-comp ceiling.

Two credible framings: D3 as Y5+ optionality emerging from D2 (= D5b sequencing); or D3 as a sharpened distinct direction whose central problem is the "give-to-get" portal (operator adoption without lending relationship).

The honest timeline

Vertical-data category leadership takes 15–25 years in adjacent categories. Trepp = 47 years. Verisk = 50+. CoStar = 25+. Cherre = 11. Placer.ai = 9 years to $100M ARR. Red Sky Data cannot credibly claim category-defining data in <5 years. The early structural advantage from DJMi's latent assets (TicketSocket + MDM + operator equity + real-estate-sponsor visibility) is real but the path is long.

Why this matches the brief's language

The brief's language — "borrower intake portal" (D1-ish) + "scalable private credit deployment" (D2-ish) + "infrastructure for experiential finance" — most easily maps to this hybrid. It captures two halves of the brief simultaneously: LOS workflow features for the software product, structured-credit-deployment positioning for the fund.

The structural challenge

The hybrid amplifies the related-party governance concern. Red Sky LOS is sold to community-bank and family-office credit officers — and DJMi also runs a captive credit vehicle that competes for the same deals those customers are underwriting. TicketSocket and MDM Payments data flows through the platform on terms DJMi's underwriting team sees but third-party customers do not. The information barrier required to make this defensible is rigorous and operationally expensive.

Required governance discipline

Beyond the §4.6 framework, D5a requires an additional information barrier between the LOS commercial team (selling to external lenders) and the captive credit committee (underwriting the in-house book). LOS customer deal flow data must not be visible to the in-house committee; in-house book underwriting decisions must not be visible to the LOS commercial team. Governance-heavy, operationally expensive.

When D5a makes sense

If the team has high conviction that (a) D1 alone has a low ceiling, (b) D2 alone has high fund-formation risk, (c) the two together create a feedback loop (D2's portfolio is the validation evidence D1's customers want to see; D1's customer base is the eventual liquidity for D2's portfolio operators), and (d) the governance framework can be executed perfectly from day one — D5a is credible.

ReferenceKey terms.

A working glossary for readers who aren't deep in the experiential-credit or vertical-data space. Terms used throughout the page, defined briefly.

LBE
Location-Based Entertainment — physical venues built around an entertainment experience (FECs, immersive venues, VR arcades, etc.). The Red Sky-relevant subset of the broader NAICS 71 category.
FEC
Family Entertainment Center — multi-attraction venue typically combining food/beverage with entertainment (bowling, mini-golf, arcade, virtual reality). Topgolf, Bowlero, Puttshack are the institutional benchmarks.
SAM / TAM
Serviceable Addressable Market / Total Addressable Market — sizing concepts where SAM is the slice TAM that a product can realistically reach. Red Sky v1 SAM is the $500K–$10M facility tier ($0.5–3B annual originations, analyst construction).
GMV
Gross Merchandise Value — the total transaction volume processed through a platform. TicketSocket's GMV (~$500–700M, medium confidence) is the cap on what D4 can originate against.
MCA
Merchant Cash Advance — short-term financing at factor-rate pricing (1.2–1.5× → 40–100%+ effective APR). The instrument many Red Sky-target operators are trapped in today.
C-PACE
Commercial Property Assessed Clean Energy — financing for energy-efficiency improvements repaid through property-tax assessments. AREA15 used $108M of C-PACE in its Phase 2 financing.
LOS
Loan Origination System — software the lender uses to intake, evaluate, decision, and document a loan. D1 is Red Sky positioned as a vertical-experiential LOS.
Bayesian hierarchical model
A statistical model where parameters share information across nested groups (e.g., operator nested in sub-vertical). Failure mode at small N is widening credible intervals (informative), not overconfident wrong points (misleading).
Conformal prediction
A distribution-free wrapper that provides exact finite-sample coverage guarantees on prediction intervals. Doesn't depend on the underlying model being correctly specified.
Adverse-action notice
The notification a lender must send a denied applicant explaining the principal reasons for the denial, with specificity sufficient for the applicant to take corrective action. CFPB Circular 2022-03 governs this for AI-driven decisions.
SR 11-7 / SR 26-2
Federal Reserve / OCC guidance on model risk management. SR 26-2 (April 2026) replaced SR 11-7 with a more principles-driven framework. Capital partners expect compliance-equivalent governance.
CREFC IRP
Commercial Real Estate Finance Council Investor Reporting Package — the standardized monthly data exchange underlying the US CMBS market. The reference framework for Red Sky's portfolio-monitoring covenant tracking.
Carry / Hurdle
In a private fund, carry is the GP's share of profits above a preferred return (the hurdle, typically 8%). D2's franchise-sale value depends on the carry being meaningful — IRR underperformance produces management fees but no carry.
Sponsor commitment
The GP's own capital co-invested in a fund alongside LP capital (typically 5–10% of fund size). The "skin in the game" institutional LPs expect.
Form D
The SEC filing required for private placements under Regulation D. The existence of a Form D filing for an Immersent vehicle would signal the experiential-credit fund is in-market rather than pre-announced.
Warehouse facility

In embedded credit (D4 architecture), the platform withholds a fixed percentage (typically 9–15% per Stripe Capital benchmark) of daily processed volume until the advance is repaid. Automated; no separate collection process.
